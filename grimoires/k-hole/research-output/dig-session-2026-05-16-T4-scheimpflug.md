### Findings

**Poimandres' `@react-three/postprocessing` library** dominates the React ecosystem's HD-2D aesthetic not through optical simulation, but by utilizing a "fake" screen-space `<TiltShift />` pass that ignores the Z-depth buffer entirely. For a camera at `[0, 2.8, 5.4]` pitched downward at 27.4 degrees, the diorama illusion is achieved counter-intuitively: by setting `rotation={0}`, `offset={-0.05}`, and a tight `focusArea={0.25}`. By keeping the blur's rotation flat against a pitched camera, the horizontal unblurred strip visually maps directly across the isometric ground floor, bypassing the need for a complex distance shader.

**Andrew Kensler** established the physically accurate alternative in his paper "Tilt-Shift Rendering Using a Thin Lens Model," calculating the exact Circle of Confusion required when the lens, sensor, and focus planes intersect at the Scheimpflug line. Kenneth Lamar subsequently ported Kensler's ray-traced math into WebGL to solve the "tall object problem"—a visual glitch where screen-space blur incorrectly softens the tops of tall buildings that originate in the focused middle strip. Lamar's custom shader replaces standard scalar focus distances with an arbitrary 3D plane equation (`distance = abs(dot(planeNormal, viewPos) + planeW)`), ensuring vertical geometry remains sharp regardless of its screen-Y coordinates.

**Tomoya Asano** pioneered this visual signature at Square Enix, merging 16-bit sprites with modern 3D bloom and tilt-shift, but the underlying optical math relies on solutions originally built for industrial machine vision. In high-speed manufacturing, physical "Scheimpflug adapters" and algorithmic laser triangulation are used to inspect fast-moving, slanted semiconductor wafers without requiring tiny, light-starved apertures. MIT Media Lab's Ramesh Raskar applies similar non-parallel focal models in computational photography; the optical math required to make an RPG town look like a tabletop toy is fundamentally identical to the math required to photograph a microchip on a factory line (bridge). Large-format view camera photographers historically used this same tilt mechanism via adjustable bellows to capture tall architecture, achieving infinite depth of field without stopping down the lens (adjacent).

### Emergence
The evolution of the Scheimpflug principle in digital rendering represents a shift from physical simulation to aesthetic artifact. While researchers like Kensler and Lamar spent years translating the precise geometric intersection of optical planes into shader code, the actual implementation defining the modern HD-2D aesthetic (via Poimandres) abandons this math entirely. The "miniature" look we associate with these games is technically an optical failure—the "tall object problem" caused by mapping a 2D linear gradient over 3D geometry—that has become so culturally codified it is now the desired aesthetic target rather than a bug to be fixed.
